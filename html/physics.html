Now, moving on for the interested reader, I like to break the notion of quantum cryptography up into a few different groups, namely:

  1. Quantum Algorithms (QA)
  2. Quantum Key Distribution (QKD)
  3. Quantum Resistant Algorithms (QRA)

Quantum Algorithms (QA)

As quantum algorithm is an algorithm that exploits quantum features, such as the interference of qubits, to gain a computational advantage. In general, they're only truly useful when executed on a quantum computer but many can be modelled with a classical computer. A unique feature of quantum algorithm is that they are not deterministic - they are probabilistic! This means that if an algrithm is ran twice for the same input, it may not produce the same output. If the algorithm is good, there is only a high probability you will receive the correct answer*. The best way to further describe a quantum algorithm is probably through example, so let's just jump in and describe Shor's Algorithm.

*Some quantum algorithms can indeed be cast in a deterministic form. See Gershenfeld and Chuang and Cory, Fahmy, and Havel.

Shor's Algorithm

In essence, Shor's algorithm provides a method for breaking group-based cryptosystems, such as RSA. The idea is to compute and store powers of a group element \(a \in \mathbb{Z^{*}_n}\) to give rise to the order of the element, \(r\), by aid of the Fourier Transform.

Quantum Resistant Algorithms (QRA)

Also referred to as post-quantum cryptography, quantum resistant algorithms are encryption schemes that are believed to be secure assuming the existence of a quantum computer.
