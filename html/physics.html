Physics

Anyone who knows me knows that I love physics. The exactness about it, the intricacy. Take the simple example of a speeding car trying to stop before it hits another car some distance in front of it; what is the minimum distance the speeding car must be from the second car before applying the brakes without hitting it? Alternatively, if the braking distance between the two cars is known a priori, what is the maximum speed the first car can achieve before it will be unable to stop without colliding with the second car? Just a basic understanding of physics and math allows us to answer these questions fairly easily.

1. Light: A Quantitative Glance

There are many different ways we can talk about light, and some are better than others depending on what aspect of light we want to talk about. The following image (which was inspired by Saleh & Teich's Fundamentals of Photonics) is useful to keep in mind: I call this the "optical theory pyramid". The optical pyramid summarizes the different theories of light and how we can talk about it, with the most fundamental and "correct" theories appearing at the base of the pyramid. Let's start at the top of the pyramid with Ray Optics. When most people talk about light, they talk about it in the context of 'rays' -- a collection of straight lines from the light source to the observer. This ray model is ancient and dates back thousands of years, but still remains relevant today,

The theory of Wave Optics is much newer relative to ray optics, and it dates back to around the time that Isaac Newton was alive. Here, the description of light as rays evolved into a more sophisticated description of light as waves, which allowed for quantitative discussions of phenomena such as diffraction and interference.

It was in the nineteenth century that James Clerk Maxwell formalized Electromagnetic Optics, an evolution to the theory of wave optics. Here, the description of light as waves was formalized within the confines of vector calculus and augmented with properties known from electricty and magnetism. Electromagnetic optics describes light as a vector field, meaning that light has a magnitude and a direction defined at every point in space. Today, electromagnetic optics provides the most accurate and encompassing description of light within the limits of classical physics, that is, without incorporating quantum mechanics.

The most base model with which one can currently describe optical phenomena is Quantum Optics. Here, Maxwell's electromagnetic field is quantized, that is, light is decribed by discrete entities known as photons rather than continuous fields. While the notion of a photon was introduced by Einstein as a 'quanta of light' in 1905, it wasn't until 1926 that the word photon was coined by chemist Gilbert Lewis. This description of light offers more than the electromagnetic field description as it is able to describe light-matter interactions.

Ray Optics

Ray optics, or geometrical optics, is one of the first things a student of optics will learn because it is the most intuitive. Ray optics is concerned with the position and momentum (direction) of light rays, and ray optics plays by a few rules:

  1. Light travels in the form of rays (duh).
  2. A substance through which light can propagate (i.e., an optical medium) is characterized by the quantity $n \geq 1$ known as the refractive index. The refractive index is the ratio for how quickly light travels in vacuum (empty space) relative to the optical medium.
  3. The optical path length, $OPL$, is defined as the product of the distance $d$ light travels through a medium and the refractive index $n$ of the medium, $ OPL = nd $. If the refractive index is spatially variant throughout the optical medium, i.e., $n = n(x, y, z)$, then the optical path length takes a line integral form: $$ OPL = \int_{\mathcal{C}} n(x,y,z) ds, $$ where $ds$ is a differential line element along the curve $\mathcal{C}$.
  4. The path that light takes through a medium is always the one that minimizes (and in some cases, maximizes) the time spent within the medium. This is known as Fermat's Principle.
With these postulates in hand, ray optics can be used to prove the Law of Reflection, Law of Refraction (Snell's Law), and many other 'laws' to do with imaging and lenses. In fact, ray optics is especially useful in determining how and where objects will appear when viewed with lenses and mirrors. I came across a cool app made from the folks at the University of Colorado at Boulder that demonstrates some basic modeling that can be done with ray optics and lens with respect to image formation.
